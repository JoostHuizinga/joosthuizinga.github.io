Research
I am a Ph. D. student at the University of Wyoming and I am working in the Evolving Artificial Intelligence Laboratory led by Jeff Clune. My research interests are in Evolutionary Algorithms, Neural Networks, Evolutionary Robotics, Learning Algorithms and almost every other aspect of Artificial Intelligence.

The Evolving Artificial Intelligence Laboratory
Work is done as part of the Evolving Artificial Intelligence Laboratory led by dr. Jeff Clune. The lab consists of bright and creative Ph. D., Master, and Undergraduate students who are all working on different areas in our field such as the evolution of hierarchically structures, the relation between structure and learning, the properties of novelty based evolution and more, but who all have the mutual goal of making computers and robots smarter through the use of evolution.
Evolving Artificial Intelligence
In the Evolving Artificial Intelligence Lab we use the principle of Evolution to help us produce smarter computers and robots. To do this we start with a set of randomly generated artificial brains which will be the individuals of our experiment. We then test these individuals on a specific task, for example we might place one of these artificial brains inside a simulated quadruped and measure how far the quadruped walks. Those individuals that perform better than their colleagues are allowed to produce offspring. The offspring is either a combination of two parents or a clone of the parent to which we then apply some minor mutations. The idea is that the minor change might improve the individuals performance making it better than its parents and thus, very slowly, helping the evolutionary process solve the task. In the last step we will select the next generation from among both parents and children based on their performance such that the total population size stays the same and from there we continue the process, generation after generation, until the problem is solved or until some predetermined number of generations.
Current Research
My current research tries to make these artificial brains look more like natural brains by mimicking the modular and regular structures found in natural brains. To do this I combine an algorithm known as HyperNEAT, which is known to generate regular structures, with an evolutionary cost for having longer connections, which was shown to produce more modular structures. The hope is that these modular and regular brains are more evolvable, that is, that it is easier for evolution to build upon these structured brains in order to produce more intelligence.
